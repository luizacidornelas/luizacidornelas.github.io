Mobility and Justice in Brasília

Ethical and policy-oriented analysis of accessibility and social exclusion

Project overview

This project examines mobility in Brasília as an ethical issue, analysing how accessibility inequalities are closely connected to territorial segregation and institutional planning decisions. The study approaches transportation not only as a technical system, but as a mechanism that shapes access to opportunities and life chances.

Why Brasília?

Brasília is a planned city conceived as a symbol of modernity. However, its urban form resulted in a highly segregated territory, where employment and public services are concentrated in central areas while low-income populations were displaced to distant peripheral regions.

Long distances, limited territorial integration, and car-oriented design make mobility a determining factor in daily life, turning place of residence into a key constraint on access to education, jobs, and healthcare.

Theoretical framework

The analysis combines the concept of social exclusion with principles of distributive justice inspired by Rawlsian egalitarianism and the Capability Approach. Together, these perspectives allow mobility inequalities to be evaluated based on their impact on real opportunities and individual freedoms.

Core ethical question:
To what extent is it acceptable for a person’s place of residence to determine their access to opportunities and future life chances?

Main insights

  • Mobility inequalities translate into inequality of opportunities.
  • Territorial segregation reinforces social exclusion.
  • Car-oriented planning disproportionately disadvantages low-income groups.
  • Justice in transportation requires prioritising disadvantaged populations.

Why this matters

When transportation policies fail to address structural inequalities, they cease to mitigate exclusion and begin to reinforce it. Justice in mobility therefore demands that access to the city be treated as a shared condition rather than a privilege.

Developed as part of the course Transportation Ethics, Politecnico di Milano (2025).
